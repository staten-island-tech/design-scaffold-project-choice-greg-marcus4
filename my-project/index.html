Coq au vin is a dish served with wine. Chicken cooked with wine, lardons, mushrooms, and optionally garlic is a French meal. Coq au vin jaune (Jura), coq au riesling (Alsace), coq au pourpre or coq au violet (Beaujolais nouveau), coq au Champagne, and other regional variations of the dish are popular.

Cassolet

Cassoulet and cognates with Spanish: cazoleta and Catalan: cassolet) is a rich, slow-cooked casserole originating in southern France and containing meat (typically pork sausages, goose, duck, and occasionally mutton), pork skin (couennes), and white beans (haricots blancs). The casserole, a deep, circular, earthenware saucepan with slanting sides, is the typical cooking vessel for it.

Transportation


France has some of the oldest transportation systems. It one of the densest networks in the world, with 146 kilometers of roads and 6.2 kilometers of rail lines per 100 kilometers squared. The center of it is in one of the most busy cities, the city of Paris. Railroads, roads, air, and water, all sorts of transportation are widely developed in France.


1. Railroads

Railroads in France have been used since 1827. They have been used for many various purposes, such as transporting goods, transporting soldiers, and helping people get around. The network has undergone a major modernization since 1981 with the arrival of the TGV high-speed rail service which has been consistently expanded in following years. They can go up to 300 km/h (186 mph) and are the pride of France's railway technology. It is currently the second largest European railway network, with a total of 29,901 kilometers of railway, and more being added each day.


2. Roads

The autoroute, also known as highway or motorway, is a network of roads over 11,882 kilometers (7,383 miles). The road signs are fundamentally different from what other countries have. They don't have a numbering system, but instead have a clustering of Autoroute numbers based on the region. Such examples include A1 being the the "autoroute du Nord" and A4 being the "autoroute de l'Est". France is believed to be the most car-dependent country, with roads spanning all over the country. There are over ~950,000 km (590,000 mi) of roads in France, which are used everyday by millions of people. They go in many different vehicles, such as their own personal cars and buses. There are also autoroutes, which some privately owned and consists of a lot of road tolls, excluding areas near large cities and in parts of the North. It has a network totalling 12,000 km (7,500 mi) of motorways. France as well also is known to have the 8th largest highway network, right behind the United States, China, Russia, Japan, Canada, Spain and Germany.


3. Waterways

The natural and man-made waterways of France are the largest in the entire continent of Europe. They expand through 8,500 kilometers (5,300 miles) of France and are mostly managed by the French navigation authority. Such examples would include the Loire, Seine and Rhône. Tourists are able to cruise through many of them and enjoy the scenery.
