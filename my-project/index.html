Attractions

The Eiffel Tower

It was built from 1887 to 1889 as the centerpiece of the 1889 World's Fair and was initially condemned for its design by some of France's top artists and philosophers, but it has since become a global cultural icon of France and one of the most recognizable structures in the world. The Eiffel Tower is the most visited paid-entry monument in the world, with 6.91 million visitors in 2015. In 1964, the Tower was designated as a historic monument, and in 1991, it was included as part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site "Paris, Banks of the Seins." The tower is 330 meters (1,083 feet) tall, about the same as an 81-story building, and it is the tallest structure in the world The tower is 330 meters (1,083 feet) tall, about the same as an 81-story skyscraper, and it is Paris' highest structure. It has a square foundation that measures 125 meters (410 feet) on each side. The Eiffel Tower eclipsed the Washington Monument during construction to become the world's highest man-made structure, a title it held for 41 years until the Chrysler Building in New York City was completed in 1930. It was the first structure in the world to reach heights of more than 200 meters and 300 meters.

Musée du Louvre

The Louvre, often known as the Louvre Museum, is a historic monument in Paris, France, and the world's most visited museum. It houses some of the world's most famous works of art, like as the Mona Lisa and the Venus de Milo. It is located on the Right Bank of the Seine in the city's 1st arrondissement and is a civic landmark (district or ward). Approximately 38,000 pieces from prehistory to the twenty-first century are on display at any given moment, covering an area of 72,735 square meters (782,910 square feet).The museum is housed in the Louvre Palace, which was erected by Philip II in the late 12th and early 13th centuries. In the museum's basement, remnants of the medieval Louvre fortress can be seen. The stronghold soon lost its defensive function as a result of urban expansion, and Francis I converted it into the French Kings' royal home in 1546. [6] The current Louvre Palace was built after the structure was enlarged several times. Louis XIV chose the Palace of Versailles for his household in 1682, leaving the Louvre primarily as a display space for the royal collection, which included ancient Greek and Roman sculpture from 1692.

Château de Versailles

The Palace of Versailles is a former royal home in Versailles, France, located about 12 miles (19 kilometers) west of Paris. The French Republic owns the palace, which has been managed by the Public Establishment of the Palace, Museum, and National Estate of Versailles since 1995, under the leadership of the French Ministry of Culture. Every year, 15,000,000 people visit the Palace, Park, and Gardens of Versailles, making it one of the world's most popular tourist destinations. In 1623, Louis XIII constructed a primitive hunting lodge on the site of the Palace of Versailles, which was rebuilt in 1631–34 by a tiny château. From 1661 to 1715, Louis XIV transformed the château into a palace in stages. Both kings favored it, and in 1682, Louis XIV moved the seat of his court and government to Versailles, effectively making the palace the de facto capital of France. Kings Louis XV and Louis XVI maintained this state of affairs, mostly making interior changes to the palace, but the royal family and capital of France returned to Paris in 1789.

Cité de Carcassonne

The Cité de Carcassonne is a medieval fortress located in the French city of Carcassonne, in the Aude department, Occitanie region. It is located on a hill on the right bank of the Aude River in the city's southeast section. Because of its unique witness to the architecture and design of a medieval fortified town, the citadel was rebuilt at the end of the 19th century and inscribed to the UNESCO list of World Heritage Sites in 1997.The citadel was built during the Gallo-Roman period and is known for its three-kilometer-long double encircling walls with 52 towers. The town has a 2,500-year history and has been occupied by Romans, Visigoths, and Crusaders at various times. It was a Gaulish community at the start of its existence, but in the third century AD, the Romans opted to turn it into a walled town. By 333 AD, when the settlement was characterized as a castellum, the Roman defenses were in place. The original walls were supported by between 34 and 40 towers, spaced from 18 to 30 metres apart along the curtain wall. Each tower was semicircular in plan and around 14 metres tall.With several construction projects, Bernard Aton IV Trencavel, vicomte of Albi, Nîmes, and Béziers, brought prosperity to the city. In Languedoc, a new sect known as Catharism arose around this time. With Pope Urban II's blessing, the Viscount of Trencavel authorized the construction of the Basilica of Saint-Nazaire in 1096. In 1107, the citizens revolted against his rule and petitioned Count Ramon Berenguer III of Barcelona to depose him. Bernard Aton regained control of the Cité with the support of Bertrand, Count of Toulouse. A second insurrection occurred in 1120, but Bernard Aton restored control a few years later. In 1130, he began work on a palace for himself as well as the rebuilding of Gallo-Roman defenses. 

Cathédrale Notre-Dame de Chartres

Chartres Cathedral, also known as the Cathedral of Our Lady of Chartres (French: Cathédrale Notre-Dame de Chartres), is the seat of the Bishop of Chartres and is located in Chartres, France, some 80 kilometers (50 miles) southwest of Paris. It lies on the site of at least five cathedrals that have occupied the site since the Diocese of Chartres was constituted as an episcopal see in the 4th century. It was mostly built between 1194 and 1220. It's in the styles of High Gothic and Romanesque. In 1979, UNESCO designated the cathedral as a World Heritage Site, calling it "the pinnacle of French Gothic art" and a "masterpiece." The cathedral has been well-preserved and renovated, with the bulk of the original stained glass windows intact and only minimal changes to the building since the early 13th century. The exterior of the building is dominated by heavy flying buttresses, which allowed the architects to significantly increase the window size, while the west end is dominated by two contrasting spires a 105-metre (349-foot) plain pyramid completed around 1160 and a 113-metre (377-foot) early 16th-century Flamboyant spire on top of an older tower. The three huge façades, each embellished with hundreds of carved figures depicting significant theological themes and histories, are also noteworthy. The cathedral has been a popular tourist site since at least the 12th century. The layout, like that of other Gothic cathedrals, is in the shape of a cross and was influenced by the shape and size of the 11th-century Romanesque cathedral beneath it, which has a crypt and remnants. At the western end, a two-bay narthex leads to a seven-bay nave that leads to the crossing, from which wide transepts extend three bays to the north and south. Four rectangular bays end in a semicircular apse east of the crossing. Single aisles flank the nave and transepts, widening to a double-aisled ambulatory around the choir and apse. Three deep semi-circular chapels radiate from the ambulatory (overlying Fulbert's 11th-century crypt's deep chapels).The elevation was bolder and more unique than the floor design, especially to the use of the flying buttress to support the top walls.

Parc National Des Calanques

Calanques National Park (French: Parc national des Calanques) is a French national park in Bouches-du-Rhône, Southern France, located on the Mediterranean coast. It was founded in 2012 and spans 520 km2 (201 sq mi), with 85 km2 (33 sq mi) of land and the rest being marine.[1] It includes parts of the Massif des Calanques, which runs between Marseille, Cassis, and La Ciotat.The Comité de défense des Calanques was founded in 1923 with the goal of limiting the development of industry in En-Vau. The groupement d'intérêt public (GIP) des Calanques was established in 1999 to prepare for the establishment of a national park. The GIP presented its first draft for a national park eleven years later, and the third draft was authorized in 2011. Prime Minister François Fillon signed the proclamation establishing Calanques National Park on April 18, 2012.

Coq Au Vin

Coq au vin is a dish served with wine. Chicken cooked with wine, lardons, mushrooms, and optionally garlic is a French meal. Coq au vin jaune (Jura), coq au riesling (Alsace), coq au pourpre or coq au violet (Beaujolais nouveau), coq au Champagne, and other regional variations of the dish are popular.

Cassolet

Cassoulet and cognates with Spanish: cazoleta and Catalan: cassolet) is a rich, slow-cooked casserole originating in southern France and containing meat (typically pork sausages, goose, duck, and occasionally mutton), pork skin (couennes), and white beans (haricots blancs). The casserole, a deep, circular, earthenware saucepan with slanting sides, is the typical cooking vessel for it. 

Bœuf bourguignon

Beef bourguignon, also known as beef Burgundy and buf à la Bourguignonne, is a French beef stew made with carrots, onions, garlic, and a bouquet garni, and topped with pearl onions, mushrooms, and bacon. Piece de boeuf à la bourguignonne is a similar recipe that uses a piece of braised beef with the same garnish.

Chocolate soufflé

A soufflé is a baked egg-based delicacy that dates from the early eighteenth century in France. It can be served as a savory main meal or sweetened as a dessert when combined with other ingredients. The French verb souffler means "to blow," "to breathe," "to inflate," or "to puff," and the word soufflé is the past participle of that verb.

Flamiche

Flamiche is a typical French pie made with chopped leeks, a little butter, and either milk or crème fraîche. Picardy's version of the famed Lorraine quiche, the dish has its origins in the French area of Picardy. The earliest recipe was discovered in a French soldier's diary in the late 18th century. Flamiche is now available in a variety of flavors.

Confit De Canard

Duck confit (French: confit de canard) is a whole duck dish prepared in France. All of the duck pieces are utilized to make the supper in Gascony, according to the families who are carrying on the duck confit heritage.

Tarte Tatin

The tarte Tatin (French pronunciation: [tat tat]) is a pastry in which the fruit (typically apples) is caramelized in butter and sugar before the tart is baked, named after the Tatin sisters who devised it and served it as their hotel's signature dish. It started in France, but has now expanded to other countries.

Ratatouille

Ratatouille is a stewed vegetable dish from the French Provençal region of Nice, often known as ratatouille niçoise. Tomato, garlic, onion, courgette (zucchini), aubergine (eggplant), capsicum (bell pepper), and some combination of lush green herbs native to the region are frequent ingredients in recipes and cooking periods.

Transportation

France has some of the oldest transportation systems. It one of the densest networks in the world, with 146 kilometers of roads and 6.2 kilometers of rail lines per 100 kilometers squared. The center of it is in one of the most busy cities, the city of Paris. Railroads, roads, air, and water, all sorts of transportation are widely developed in France.

Railroads

Railroads in France have been used since 1827. They have been used for many various purposes, such as transporting goods, transporting soldiers, and helping people get around. The network has undergone a major modernization since 1981 with the arrival of the TGV high-speed rail service which has been consistently expanded in following years. They can go up to 300 km/h (186 mph) and are the pride of France's railway technology. It is currently the second largest European railway network, with a total of 29,901 kilometers of railway, and more being added each day.

Roads

France is believed to be the most car-dependent country, with roads spanning all over the country. There are over ~950,000 km (590,000 mi) of roads in France, which are used everyday by millions of people. They go in many different vehicles, such as their own personal cars and buses. There are also autoroutes, which some privately owned and consists of a lot of road tolls, excluding areas near large cities and in parts of the North. It has a network totalling 12,000 km (7,500 mi) of motorways. France as well also is known to have the 8th largest highway network, right behind the United States, China, Russia, Japan, Canada, Spain and Germany.

Waterways

The beautiful, natural, and man-made waterways of France are the largest in the entire continent of Europe. They expand through 8,500 kilometers (5,300 miles) of France and are mostly managed by the French navigation authority. Such examples would include the Loire, Seine and Rhône. Tourists are able to cruise through many of them to enjoy the nature and scenery.

Marine Transport

France has a large commercial marine, with 55 ships with a gross register tonnage of 1,000 tons or more. French companies also have a total of 1,400 ships, 700 which are registered with France. The 110 shipping firms employ 12,500 personel at sea and 15,500 on shore. Every year, 305 million tonnes of goods and 15 million passengers are shipped to sea to a destination somewhere in the world. France also boasts a large number of seaports, which include: Bayonne, Bordeaux, Boulogne-sur-Mer, Brest, Calais, Cherbourg-Octeville, Dunkerque, Fos-sur-Mer, La Pallice, Le Havre, Lorient, Marseille, Nantes, Nice, Paris, Port-la-Nouvelle, Port-Vendres, Roscoff, Rouen, Saint-Nazaire, Saint-Malo, Sète, Strasbourg and Toulon.

Hotels

Four Seasons Hotel George V, Paris

The Hotel George V, named after the United Kingdom's King George V, first opened its doors in 1928. It was financed by American billionaire Joel Hillman for $31 million (60 million Francs). André Terrail, owner of La Tour d'Argent, was in charge of the operation. Georges Wybo, who also created the Casino de Deauville and the renovation of the Printemps Haussmann after the fire of 1921, was its architect. The hotel was popular with Americans arriving in France on Transatlantic ocean liners because it had offices in the port of Cherbourg where they could be met.

Waldorf Astoria Versailles - Trianon Palace

Since 1910, the Trianon Palace has been a five-star venue with spectacular neoclassical architecture. You will get a true sense of place thanks to its grand gallery, which is lit by natural light and designed with a subtle and contemporary touch. This historic hotel has 85 rooms and 15 suites with spectacular views of the park of Versailles or the hotel's grounds. The restaurants and bar, as well as the famed Spa Guerlain and the Clemenceau Ballroom, are all housed within one structure.

Shangri-La Paris

The old residence of Prince Roland Bonaparte, which overlooks the Eiffel Tower and the Seine, exemplifies ultimate Parisian grandeur. The "Palais" at No.10 avenue d'Iéna was reincarnated as Shangri-La Paris in December 2010 after being meticulously restored to its previous splendor. The hotel blends European grandeur with traditional Asian hospitality and is centrally positioned in the 16th arrondissement, minutes from the Champs-Elysées.

Hotel Eiffel Blomet

The new-look Hotel Eiffel Blomet is ready to greet you after major renovations and refurbishing. The hotel is a short distance from the Eiffel Tower, and its revival corresponds with that of its neighbor, the legendary Bal Blomet, which reopens in spring 2017. In its heyday, the famed venue was known as the "bal Nègre," and it housed the icons of those "crazy years," when the Charleston was danced to the latest jazz sounds.

Hôtel Martinez - In the Unbound Collection by Hyatt

Hôtel Martinez, which first opened its doors in the late 1920s, represents the Côte d'Azur's lightheartedness. This is and was the spot to see and be seen on the famed Croisette, thanks to its welcoming atmosphere and love for life.
